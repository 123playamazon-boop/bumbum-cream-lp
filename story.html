I Gave Away My Favorite Bikini Three Summers Ago — Because Of What The Backs Of My Thighs Looked Like From Behind

My name is Rachel Alvarez. I’m 41. I sell real estate in Scottsdale, and I spend my days being looked at — open houses, client dinners, listing photos where I’m the one smiling by the front door.

I’ve had cellulite and a bottom that started to fall since my second baby. Not the faint kind that only shows if you pinch the skin. The dimpled, deflated kind that’s there when I stand, when I sit, when I do absolutely nothing. Worst on the backs of my thighs. The kind that, in shorts or a swimsuit, anyone standing behind me could see.

The day I stopped pretending was a photo from a lake weekend in July. Sunset. A group of us laughing at something I can’t even remember now. She posted twelve pictures. The one she tagged me in was from behind.

You could see everything.

I run a business. My hair is done, my nails are done, my car costs more than my first house. And the backs of my thighs looked like the surface of a golf ball. I closed the app, walked to the mirror, turned around, and looked over my shoulder at a view I’d been hiding from my entire adult life.

The next morning, I started buying things.

I Tried Everything. I Have The Receipts.

Nivea Q10 firming. Every morning and night for three months. The cellulite did not move.

Bliss FatGirlSlim. Thirty-eight dollars, “active sculpting complex.” Four months. My thighs smelled like coffee. Same dimples.

Sol de Janeiro Brazilian Bum Bum cream. Forty-five dollars. Everyone on TikTok swore by it, so I used it every day for five months. My skin smelled incredible. My bottom looked exactly the same. Keep that one in mind — it was the most important mistake I made, and almost nobody understands why.

Dry brushing for six months. Coffee scrubs. A $200 vibration plate. A gallon of water a day. Cutting salt. Cutting sugar. Apple cider vinegar.

Nothing.

Then I Did The Math On The Clinic. And Priced Out The Surgery.

I found a lymphatic-drainage clinic — robes, cucumber water, the whole thing. $120 a session. By the fourth session my thighs looked genuinely smoother. The therapist told me I’d have to come every week to keep it.

I did the math standing at her front desk. $120 a week. $6,240 a year. To rent smoother skin — because if I missed one week, I could see the difference by day five.

So I priced the permanent fix. A BBL. $12,000. Then I read what nobody puts in the brochure: surgical drains taped to your hips for two weeks, sleeping face-down on a foam wedge for two months, three out of four women calling the recovery “worse than childbirth.”

I closed the laptop. And then I just… gave up. Longer shorts. One-pieces. The bikini I loved went into my sister’s closet and I never replaced it. For a year, I hid.

“Brazilian women don’t use firming cream. That’s an American thing. We use a modelador.”— the sentence that finally changed it for me

Then A Brazilian Friend Looked At My Bathroom Shelf And Laughed

Her name is Marina. São Paulo. We were packing for a work trip and she saw my army of firming creams — the Nivea, the Bliss, the $45 Bum Bum — and said the thing I can’t stop repeating: “We don’t use firming cream. We use a modelador.”

I’d never heard the word. That’s the whole point. A modelador is an entire product category — the one Brazilian women have used daily for 30 years — that American brands skipped on purpose, because the rules on what a “firming” cream can legally claim made the stronger category too awkward to sell here. So we got sold the weak version. The lotion. The one that just smells nice.

That’s why the $45 Bum Bum cream did nothing for me. It was never built to firm anything. It’s a gorgeous fragranced moisturizer that sits on the surface. And cellulite and sag don’t live on the surface.

Here’s What The $59 Gadgets Get Right — And Why You Don’t Need One

The suction devices and the lymphatic clinics are right about one thing: the problem is three layers down. Cellulite and loose skin form in the deep layer, where fibrous bands pull the skin down and firmness is lost. A drugstore lotion physically stops at the top layer. It can’t reach the problem. That’s not your fault — that’s chemistry.

The modelador reaches it. The active is guaraná — 2× the caffeine of coffee — and topical caffeine is one of the only ingredients with published research for the look of firmer skin and less-visible cellulite (a 2025 clinical evaluation in Cosmetics, MDPI, tested a topical caffeine cream on 56 women with moderate-to-severe cellulite, twice daily for 8 weeks). It drives the actives into the exact layer that $6,240-a-year clinic was working with her hands — except it lives in my bathroom cabinet and costs about a dollar a day.

Sixty seconds. Once a day. After the shower. No needle. No $59 machine. No weekly appointment.
